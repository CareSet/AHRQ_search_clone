Importance of Patient-Reported Outcomes for Quality Improvement

By: Darren A. DeWalt, MD, MPH and Dennis A. Revicki, PhD

  • Share
    • Facebook
    • Twitter
    • Linkedin
    • Email
  • Print
  • Read Comments (0)

Although much of health care is focused on increasing longevity, many of our treatments are specifically designed to improve symptoms and function, two essential components of health-related quality of life. In many cases, the best way to measure symptoms and functional status is by direct patient query. The importance of such patient-reported outcomes (PROs) is evidenced by their increased use in clinical trials and in drug and device label claims. Effective quality improvement requires relentless focus on the outcomes. As Dr. Wachter noted in a previous commentary in this series, (1) we tend to focus on what we measure; hence, a good measure of patient-reported symptoms and function will provide a good focus for quality improvement. Because process and structure almost always inadequately describe the care needed for optimal health outcomes, a focus only on process or structure may lead to less overall health gains. Unfortunately, most quality improvement efforts do a cursory job of assessing PROs at best.

In this commentary, we discuss the National Institutes of Health (NIH)-sponsored Patient-Reported Outcome Measurement Information System (PROMIS) initiative and concerns about PRO measurement in quality improvement, and we suggest two strategies for integrating PRO measurements into quality improvement. For the purposes of this commentary, PRO refers to a standardized set of questions that can provide a numerical score representing a person's status in the concept being measured (e.g., pain, fatigue, physical function, depression, etc.). We also distinguish these PROs from patient satisfaction measures. The PRO is specifically geared toward about the symptoms and function of the patient. Satisfaction measures, which are commonly used in quality measurement, are patient reported measures of an aspect of the health care system. Patient satisfaction is an important part of quality of care, but distinct from PROs and not the topic of this commentary.

The NIH PROMIS Project

The PROMIS initiative is a collaboration between the NIH and several PRO research teams. Initiated in late 2004, this 5-year project is developing and evaluating item banks and innovative methods for collecting PRO data for physical function, pain, fatigue, depression, anxiety, social function and other domains (e.g., sleep/wake function). Item response theory (IRT) is being used to evaluate and calibrate these item banks to enable flexible and efficient assessment approaches for these health domains. (2) IRT is part of modern test theory that gives us a clearer picture of the information gained from each individual item and item response, and allows us to customize sets of items to improve the overall test characteristics. Because IRT provides an understanding of how the individual item response relates to the overall characteristic being measured, we have much more flexibility to administer the right questions at the right time for the patient to answer. For example, PROMIS has developed short-form measures and a computerized adaptive testing system that allows for efficient, psychometrically robust assessment of PROs in clinical research and practice settings. The result is the development of tests closely tailored to the health status of individual patients.

The PROMIS measures are intended to be part of a publicly available system that can be added to and modified periodically and that allows researchers and health systems to access a common repository of items and computerized adaptive tests (see www.nihpromis.org  and link to Assessment Center). Several studies are underway to test the performance of the PROMIS measures in patients with rheumatoid arthritis, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, cancer, low back pain, depression, and heart failure. Plans are currently in development to establish a public-private partnership to sustain the item banks, ensure scientific excellence, improve future data collection activities, add new domains and items to the system, test and adapt the system for new populations, maintain the system in the public domain, and extend the application of the system for clinical research and practice. These advances in measurement science and administration technology can make PRO assessment a regular part of clinical practice.

PROs and Clinical Practice

PROs are often not included in routine clinical care or quality improvement activities because some clinicians believe that PROs are not "objective" measures or that they lack precision for measuring individual patients. (3) PROs are prone to bias and error, just like any clinical measure to assess health status. For instance, physical examination findings and interpretation of x-rays can be biased by lack of objectivity. Even blood tests have error and many have measurement properties similar to PROs. (4) Several studies have demonstrated that using PRO measures as part of clinical care improves outcomes. (5-9) Additionally, some studies have shown that better process of medical care is related to better health-related quality of life outcomes. (10) To improve the usability of PRO measurement in clinical care, efforts such as PROMIS are yielding precise measures that require fewer questionnaire items. For example, precise (i.e., reliable at the individual level) PROMIS domain scores are possible with as few as five items in an adaptive testing environment. Because the assessment is administered on the computer, the score is immediately available without further data entry or calculation.

When using PROs in clinical practice, the administration of a standardized questionnaire is often a barrier to implementation. Practices have used several strategies to administer PRO questionnaires, and testing platforms, such as PROMIS, are making this easier. Questionnaires can be completed by patients at home or in the waiting room, prior to the visit, using a paper or computer based administration. PROMIS also has automated interactive voice response systems that can administer the questionnaires over the telephone. Some practices have personnel administer questionnaires orally for patients with low literacy skills. Almost all questionnaires for use in clinical settings are easily scored to provide a useful and immediate measure of the patient's condition. Several models are available and can be customized to the resources available in any practice setting.

When approaching the use of PROs in quality improvement, clinicians should be clear about their hypotheses regarding changes in PROs over time. Improvement in scores may take a long time depending on the treatment. For example, patients with diabetes may not have substantial changes in physical, emotional, or social function within a year or two; one does, however, expect to detect important changes over 10 years. Alternatively, some treatments can lead to relatively rapid improvements in PROs. For example, medical treatment for depression and asthma and surgery for hip replacement often produce detectable changes in PROs within weeks to months. The Partners in Care program, focused on improving depression treatment in primary care, demonstrated improvements in both the process (percentage of patient receiving recommended therapy and/or medications) and outcomes of care (scores on PROs and percentage of patients still depressed). (8-9)

Two Stages to Integrating PROs into Quality Measurement

For some conditions, the use of PROs to support individual patient care is well proven. In these cases, quality measures have been defined by the percentage of patients who completed a PRO instrument as part of their care (a process measure). This is a reasonable first stage. Such a step helps clinical practices integrate PRO measurement into their clinical systems and is needed before practices can effectively use PROs in care management.

Current examples of such measures are the percentage of patients in primary care who are screened for depression, the percentage of patients with depression who completed a standardized questionnaire, and the percentage of patients with asthma that completed the Asthma Control Test™. (11) Of these measures, only the asthma measure (measure summary available on the NQMC Web site) has been endorsed by the National Quality Forum, and the Asthma Control Test™ is optional for meeting the standard of that measure. (12) Clinicians can use a nonstandardized assessment of symptoms to meet the requirement. Although these types of measures are being developed for specific projects, they have not yet entered the mainstream of quality measurement.

Although PRO measurement in and of itself may be necessary to improve care, it is not sufficient to prove that high-quality care took place. Moreover, determining whether PROs have been used is, in effect, evaluating only the process of care, not its outcome; that is, measuring only whether someone used a PRO is akin to asking whether the blood pressure was measured, not whether the blood pressure was kept under control or even whether an attempt was made to do so.

The second stage for implementing PROs in quality improvement is to assess changes in scores on the individual level or on the population level. For example, clinicians can monitor change in PRO scores for patients who are initiating or changing medications, or they can track changes in PROs for groups in their practices defined by diagnosis, medication, or combinations. Transitioning health services in this direction will help to ensure that services remain patient centered and that our efforts improve the way patients feel and function.

A good example of this type of measure is the "Mean of patients' change scores on the "Depression/Functioning" subscale of the BASIS-24® survey." This particular measure (measure summary available on the NQMC Web site) assesses the change in a PRO score between the start of treatment for depression and the end of a designated treatment period by taking the mean of the change score of a sample of patients. Another way to calculate a measure of this type is to identify a threshold change score constituting a clinically important difference and report the percentage of patients who achieve that score or better. Both ways of scoring are reported in the Partners in Care study. (9)

These types of measures seem especially important when assessing quality of care for patients with symptomatic conditions who are using treatments that are known to improve PROs. Certainly, any non-emergent procedure should be monitored with before-after PRO assessment to track improvements in that procedure. In addition, PRO scores for patient populations can be compared with normative data; another endpoint might be the percentage of patients within some criteria for remission or minimal symptoms.

When managing a population of patients, a clinician would typically seek to achieve the best possible PRO scores across his/her caseload. For example, a reasonable quality measure for asthma may be the percentage of patients with asthma whose symptoms are under control (minimal to no symptoms from asthma) on the Asthma Control Test™, which asks several questions about important asthma symptoms. Using this type measure, practices and health systems can take action to ensure that all patients with asthma are adequately treated to minimize symptoms. Health Disparities Collaboratives use a similar type of measure where practices report an average of symptom-free days over the population. (13)

PRO measurement can provide critically important information to quality improvement initiatives, enabling them to focus on what is most important to patients: how they feel and function. PRO measurement is advancing quickly and is the subject of international attention; for instance, papers to be published in 2008 in Quality of Life Research from a recent conference of the International Society for Quality of Life Research will document developments in applying PROs in clinical practice. (14) Moreover, initiatives such as PROMIS are improving precision, reducing respondent burden, and making PRO assessments more feasible in clinical practice. Performance measures that evaluate the use of PROs for specific conditions are emerging, but we need more integration of PRO scores as part of clinical care (process measure of quality) and into assessment of formal quality improvement efforts as outcome measures.
