Broadening the Quality Measurement Landscape: The Importance of Competence

By: Eric S. Holmboe, MD

  • Share
    • Facebook
    • Twitter
    • Linkedin
    • Email
  • Print
  • Read Comments (0)

The quality of healthcare in the U.S. remains suboptimal despite a national focus on quality problems the last 10 years (1-3). One increasingly popular strategy to accelerate quality improvement is to measure and reward individual physician performance and to report that information publicly (4-5). Physicians are often the predominant, and, at times, the only healthcare provider in many settings, especially office practices. Given that the majority of clinical care in the U.S. actually occurs in offices consisting of four or fewer physicians, with 39% of visits occurring in solo physician practices, a focus on physician performance as a lever for improving quality is logical (5). Second, research has shown that physician leadership is essential for effective quality improvement (6-7). Third, as the adage goes, we can only improve what we measure, or otherwise make visible. However, there are several challenges to using the current framework for measuring physician performance to improve quality. In this commentary, I will outline some of these key challenges and suggest that we need to broaden our conceptual model of existing performance measurement.

Successful clinical outcomes are the result of complex interactions between healthcare providers, patients and microsystems. One can think of these interactions as a "competency triangle" as shown in Figure 1. Each component in this triadic relationship must not only possess a minimal level of competence, but the interactions between the components must also be effective, timely, efficient and safe in order to achieve successful outcomes (3).

There is broad consensus that improving "systems" of care is essential to improving patient care and safety (3). The argument is that good, competent people do ineffective or harmful things when they work in dysfunctional systems. One example of an important system problem is lack of adequate information management (3,6,8). Electronic medical records are seen as an important solution to the problem of dysfunctional information management (6,8). Clearly, systems "competence" is essential in creating the appropriate practice environment, and providing the necessary tools to enable high quality care and technologies, such as electronic medical records, are certainly important components.

However, systems are more than just technology. In fact, systems are mostly about people. Nelson and colleagues' definition of a microsystem is particularly helpful in this regard: A microsystem is a "small, organized care unit with a specific clinical purpose, set of patients, technologies and multiple (healthcare) practitioners who work directly with these patients" (6). In their qualitative research studies of highly functioning microsystems, Nelson and colleagues uncovered nine key success factors: (1) information and information technology; (2) effective leadership of the microsystem; (3) macrosystem support of the microsystem; (4) strong patient focus; (5) staff focus on competence and roles; (6) interdependence of the care team; (7) ongoing process improvement; (8) education and training of all staff; and (9) performance results. Notably, most of this list involves human factors; this should come as no surprise as medicine is, in its purest form, a deeply human activity.

Three of the key success factors were staff competence, education and leadership. As my colleagues and I argued in a recent commentary, effective microsystems must have highly competent providers, including physicians, who possess sufficient knowledge and clinical skills to make and execute evidenced-base decisions, exercise informed clinical judgment, and deal effectively with uncertainty and complexity (9). Physician competence is especially important given physicians provide care directly to patients and often serve as leaders of the microsystems.

The core physician competencies have been defined by the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) and the American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS) (10): medical knowledge; patient care; professionalism; interpersonal and communication skills; practice-based learning and improvement; and systems-based practice. Among these six, medical knowledge and clinical skills (subsumed under the general competency of patient care) are essential physician competencies needed for high quality care. Unfortunately, current performance measures discount the importance of these competencies.

Competence in medical knowledge and the clinical skills of medical interviewing, physician examination, counseling and communication are all essential for effective clinical judgment and care management. Surprisingly, clinical judgment is often ignored in the current quality calculus. Most current performance measures do not actually require a physician level decision; for example, ordering a routine screening test, providing an immunization, etc. can, and often should be done effectively by others in the microsystem (6). Furthermore, almost all current performance measures assume that a correct diagnosis has already been made.

Making a correct diagnosis is the integration of the key competencies of medical knowledge and the clinical skills of medical interviewing and physical examination. Cognitive science of the last twenty years has given us a much deeper understanding of this process, and it has highlighted the lasting and critical importance of clinical skills (11). Consider the following scenario recently described in an article highlighting problems with the lack of primary care. The story was about a corporate executive who went directly to a cardiologist after he developed chest pain and ended up undergoing a series of costly ($168,000) and invasive tests; ultimately the cause of the chest pain was found to be musculoskeletal (12). The article argued that the patient would have been better served by a primary care physician in a "patient-centered medical home," -- a care model emphasizing "cost-efficient, longitudinal care that promotes a continuous healing relationship through the delivery of care in a variety of care settings according to the needs of the patient and skills of the medical provider" (13). While that may be true, I would argue that a physician who performed a more accurate medical history and physical examination, in combination with broad knowledge about the causes of chest pain, would have better served this patient through competent clinical judgment.

The final leg of the competency triangle is the patient. Too often patients are treated as variables in statistical models that need to be "risk adjusted" when measuring performance. When performance measurement involves "high stakes" decisions such as financial gain (or loss), risk adjustment is reasonable from a fairness perspective. However, it is illogical from a quality improvement perspective. Patients need competence to effectively care for themselves and make good decisions. Important patient "competencies" include health literacy, numeracy, activation, advocacy, and some understanding of the microsystems they must engage for their care. The chronic disease model is one of the best representations of the need for patient competence (14). When patients lack "competency," physicians and other members of the microsystem are morally obligated to help patients acquire needed competencies to manage their own care. Using patient "incompetence" as an excuse to not engage in quality improvement is frankly unprofessional (15). This competency perspective argues strongly for the need to more systematically involve patients in the performance measurement process about how the physician and the microsystem are helping them to enhance their own competence to care for themselves. While current patient satisfaction surveys are an important step in this direction, they should be viewed as only a beginning in patient level measurement.

Where does this leave us in the evolution of performance measurement? First, measures of physician-level performance must be expanded to include medical knowledge, clinical skill and clinical judgment. These are core competencies with considerable importance and value to the healthcare system. Accurate diagnosis and effective, efficient care management of less frequent or more complex medical conditions still matter; the absence of enough cases for statistical power or lack of adequate performance assessment shouldn't stop us from working on this "too hard box" for measurement. Patient-level measurement must also evolve. While satisfaction measures will remain important, we must also develop methods to measure patients' change in experience and competence that should result from effective interactions with healthcare providers and the microsystems patients encounter. Finally, we need new measures of accountability, effectiveness and efficiency for both micro- and macrosystems. In my view, only by addressing all three arms of the competency triangle, and the interactions between them, can we truly begin to accelerate major improvements in quality of care.

In conclusion, current performance measures do have an important role in moving the quality improvement agenda forward; one might think of them as an early and essential foundation but insufficient. If we are to accelerate improvement efforts, we need to broaden our conceptual model of performance assessment and recognize that knowledge and skills of the physicians, patients and other healthcare workers are essential and deserve greater attention in the national quality conversation.

Author

Eric S. Holmboe, MD
American Board of Internal Medicine, Philadelphia, PA

Figure 1.

Figure description: The figure, which I call the competency triangle, highlights the important triadic relationships between the physician, patient and the micro- and macrosystems of care. The physician, represented by the circle in the upper left corner, must have sufficient knowledge, skills and attitudes in the six competencies of medical knowledge, patient care, professionalism, interpersonal and communication skills, practice-based learning and improvement and systems-based practice. The patient, represented by the circle in the upper right corner, needs knowledge and skills in the competencies of health literacy, numeracy (understanding basic statistics such as proportions, percents, etc.), activation to manage their medical conditions, advocacy and coordination of their own care. The bidirectional arrow connecting the patient and physician represents the critical importance of the interaction between both parties. However, as important as the patient and physician relationship is, it is often insufficient to attain good patient outcomes. The microsystem (such as clinics and hospital wards represented as the inner circle at the bottom of the figure)and the macrosystem (represented as the outer circle around the microsystem) are also essential. Key microsystem competencies include staff competence (e.g. nurses, medical assistants, clerks, etc.), effective teamwork, information management and effective referral processes and networks. Note the bidirectional arrows between the physician and systems and the patient and systems, representing the critical importance of these interactions for effective care. Ultimately, it is the interactions of the competence of the physician, micro- and macrosystems and the patient that determine clinical outcomes.
